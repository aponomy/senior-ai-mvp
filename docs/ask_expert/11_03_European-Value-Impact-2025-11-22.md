### European Accessibility Act (EAA) - Compliance by June 2025

#### EAA Scope and Senior AI Exceeding Mandates

**EAA Mandates** accessibility for:
- E-books, e-commerce, banking services
- Computers, smart devices, operating systems
- Digital content and interfaces

**Senior AI's Approach**: **Cognitive accessibility as default, not retrofit**

**How Senior AI Exceeds EAA Requirements**:

1. **Progressive Card-Based UI**
   - Visual hierarchy optimized for cognitive processing
   - Chunking information into digestible units
   - Consistent card patterns reduce cognitive load

2. **DAG Branching that Preserves Context**
   - Non-destructive exploration reduces errors
   - Users can "try alternatives" without losing progress
   - Critical for executive function support (ADHD, MCI)

3. **Multimodal Prompts**
   - Voice + visual + text redundancy
   - Accommodates sensory impairments
   - Multiple ways to present information (ISO 21801-1 principle)

4. **Three-Column Progressive Architecture**
   - Timeline → Key Points → Full Conversation
   - Graduated information disclosure matches cognitive capacity
   - Users control depth of detail

#### EN 301 549 Conformance Plan

**Standards Compliance**:
- **EN 301 549 v3.2.1** (and updates) - European standard for ICT accessibility
- **WCAG 2.1/2.2** success criteria at AA level minimum
- **Cognitive accessibility techniques**:
  - Clear language (plain language, no jargon)
  - Step-by-step flows (task decomposition)
  - Error prevention and recovery (confirmation, undo)
  - Consistent navigation (predictable interface)
  - Alternative modalities (voice, visual, haptic)
  - Adjustable timeouts (no time pressure)
  - Large touch targets (minimum 44×44px)

**Quality Assurance**:
- Annual external accessibility audits by certified auditors
- Participatory testing with older adults and people with cognitive impairments

### Ethical AI Frameworks: HLEG Trustworthy AI Implementation

#### Seven Key Requirements (HLEG 2019)

**Detailed Implementation per Requirement**:

**1. Human Agency and Oversight**
- **Human-in-the-loop**: Escalation to human support for complex/ambiguous situations
- **User control**: Users approve high-trust actions (banking transfers, government signatures)
- **Meaningful information**: Clear explanations of AI outputs and reasoning
- **Opt-out**: Users can choose to bypass AI recommendations

**2. Technical Robustness and Safety**
- **Safe defaults**: Conservative action execution (e.g., lower transaction limits without extra verification)
- **Constrained actions**: Capability-scoped tokens (can't execute what user hasn't authorized)
- **Fallback mechanisms**: Human support available 24/7 via phone/chat
- **Population-specific testing**: Safety evaluation with older adults and cognitive disabilities
- **Adversarial robustness**: Testing against prompt injection, social engineering attempts

**3. Privacy and Data Governance**
- **Minimization**: Collect only data necessary for functionality
- **Encryption**: At-rest and in-transit encryption (AES-256, TLS 1.3)
- **Purpose limitation**: Data used only for stated purposes
- **Retention limits**: Automated deletion after user-defined or legal retention periods
- **Privacy-preserving analytics**: Aggregation, differential privacy, anonymization

**4. Transparency**
- **Plain-language explanations**: "I'm suggesting this because..." in simple terms
- **Model cards**: Documentation of AI capabilities, limitations, biases
- **Data governance transparency**: Where data goes, who has access, how it's used
- **Accessible communication**: Visual, audio, text explanations for different needs

**5. Diversity, Non-Discrimination, and Fairness**
- **Co-design**: Older adults and people with cognitive disabilities involved in design process
- **Accessibility testing**: EN 301 549 audits, user testing across disability types
- **Bias monitoring**: Performance metrics by age, gender, cognitive ability
- **Fair outcomes**: Task success rates comparable across demographic groups

**6. Societal and Environmental Well-Being**
- **Software-only**: Low carbon footprint vs hardware devices
- **EU green cloud**: Renewable energy-powered data centers
- **Social mission**: Accessible pricing (€10-20), social tariffs for low-income
- **Digital inclusion goals**: Quantified targets for underserved populations

**7. Accountability**
- **Incident logging**: All failures, errors, safety concerns documented
- **User feedback analytics**: Continuous monitoring of user-reported issues
- **Independent advisory board**: External oversight on ethics, safety, accessibility
- **Bias and safety reviews**: Quarterly assessments, public reporting
- **Redress mechanisms**: Clear process for users to challenge decisions/outcomes

**Source**: 
- HLEG Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI (European Commission, 2019)

#### Measurable Usability Metrics

**1. Task Completion Rate (TCR)**
- **Definition**: % of initiated tasks completed successfully
- **Baseline**: Standard apps/websites: 40-60% for 65+ (Nielsen Norman Group)
- **Senior AI Target**: ≥80-90% (2× improvement)
- **Measurement**: Usage analytics, A/B testing vs control interfaces

**2. Error Rate**
- **Definition**: % of tasks with user errors (wrong inputs, incorrect flows)
- **Baseline**: 20-40% for complex multi-step tasks (banking, government forms)
- **Senior AI Target**: ≤10-15% (60-70% reduction)
- **Measurement**: Error logging, undo frequency, support escalations

**3. Time-on-Task**
- **Definition**: Minutes to complete specific tasks (e.g., bank transfer, appointment booking)
- **Baseline**: 8-15 minutes for complex tasks among 65+ (HCI research)
- **Senior AI Target**: 3-6 minutes (40-50% reduction)
- **Measurement**: Session analytics, timestamp logging

**4. Step Count**
- **Definition**: Number of interactions/screens to complete task
- **Baseline**: 10-20 steps for government forms, banking flows
- **Senior AI Target**: 5-10 steps via guided workflows
- **Measurement**: Interaction logging, workflow analytics

**5. Cognitive Load (NASA-TLX Adapted)**
- **NASA-TLX**: Task Load Index measuring mental demand, temporal demand, effort
- **Adaptation**: Simplified 3-item scale for older adults
- **Baseline**: High cognitive load scores (>60/100) on complex tasks
- **Senior AI Target**: ≤40/100 (50% reduction)
- **Measurement**: Post-task surveys, longitudinal studies

**6. User Confidence Scores**
- **Definition**: Self-reported confidence in completing digital tasks
- **Scale**: 1-5 Likert ("How confident are you in managing online banking?")
- **Baseline**: 2.0-2.5 (low confidence) among digitally excluded 65+
- **Senior AI Target**: 3.5-4.0 (moderate to high confidence)
- **Measurement**: Pre/post surveys, quarterly check-ins

#### Study Design: RCTs and Pragmatic Trials

**Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)**:
- **Population**: Older adults (65+) with low digital skills
- **Intervention**: Senior AI guided workflows
- **Control**: Standard app/website interfaces OR alternative senior apps
- **Outcomes**: TCR, error rate, time-on-task, cognitive load, confidence
- **Duration**: 3-6 months minimum
- **Example**: Banking task RCT with 200 participants, comparing Senior AI vs standard banking app

**Pragmatic Trials**:
- **Real-world settings**: Municipal programs, care provider deployments
- **Cluster randomization**: By facility or municipality
- **Outcomes**: IADL scores, care utilization, institutionalization rates
- **Example**: 10 municipalities randomized to Senior AI vs usual digital services, 12-month follow-up

**EN 301 549 Cognitive Accessibility Heuristics**:
- Conformance evaluation against standards
- Expert review + user testing
- Quarterly audits and continuous improvement

**Sources**:
- Nielsen Norman Group senior usability research
- NASA-TLX (Hart & Staveland, 1988)
- EN 301 549 v3.2.1 cognitive accessibility requirements
- MRC Framework for Complex Interventions (2021)

### Measurement Methodologies

#### Study Designs

**1. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)**
- **Gold standard** for causal inference
- Individual or cluster randomization
- Outcomes: IADL, cognitive load, TCR, loneliness, well-being
- Example: 400 participants, 1:1 randomization, 6-month follow-up

**2. Cluster RCTs**
- Randomization at municipality or care provider level
- Reduces contamination
- Outcomes: Care utilization, institutionalization, costs
- Example: 20 municipalities, 10 intervention vs 10 control, 12-month follow-up

**3. Stepped-Wedge Trials**
- Progressive rollout with staggered start dates
- All clusters eventually receive intervention
- Allows within- and between-cluster comparisons
- Useful for municipal/regional deployments

**4. Pragmatic Cohort Studies**
- Real-world evidence, observational design
- Difference-in-differences using administrative data
- Outcomes: Claims data (hospitalizations, costs), eGov usage, institutionalization
- Example: Senior AI users vs matched controls using national registry data

**5. Real-World Evidence (RWE)**
- EHR/claims data analysis
- Municipal service usage data
- Aligned with HTA (Health Technology Assessment) expectations
- Cost-effectiveness analysis for payer decisions

**6. Social Return on Investment (SROI)**
- Monetizes social outcomes and externalities
- Input: Program costs
- Outputs: Economic value of independence, caregiver time, health outcomes
- SROI Ratio: Senior AI likely €8-12 per €1 invested based on estimates above

#### Standards and Frameworks

**MRC Framework for Complex Interventions (2021 update)**:
- Development → Feasibility/pilot → Evaluation → Implementation
- Process evaluation alongside outcome evaluation
- Context-mechanism-outcome analysis

**EU HTA Regulation (2021/2282)**:
- Joint clinical assessment for health technologies
- Methodological guidance for evidence generation
- Relevance for DiGA/CNEDiMTS pathways

**SROI Guide (UK Cabinet Office)**:
- Seven principles of SROI
- Stakeholder engagement, valuation of outcomes, attribution, sensitivity analysis

- **Regional**: 50-100k users (enough for regional B2B2C partnerships)

**Switching Costs** (User Perspective):
